1. Intro to the NFL Salary Cap

The NFL salary cap is a hard limit on how much money a team can spend on players' salaries in a given year. This limit affects who a team can sign (especially during free agency) and when they can sign them. Over 17 steps, you will learn about the NFL cap by signing John Doe and changing his contract. (Feel free to change John Doe's name using the input fields below.) You can navigate to the next page with the right arrow or the "N" key, and to the previous page with the left arrow or "P" key. To enter the first element in the contract area, press "E." To navigate between different contract elements, use Alt+Up Arrow and Alt+Down Arrow.

2. About the NFL Salary Cap

Unlike other leagues like the MLB, the NFL has a "hard" salary cap, meaning that no team can exceed the salary cap at any time. Therefore, the NFL does not need a luxury tax. This means that clubs must stay in cap compliance, as going over the cap can lead to the termination of contracts, loss of draft picks, and/or fines.

3. Why a Salary Cap?

The NFL has a salary cap for 2 main reasons. First, it limits how much the clubs' owners have to spend on players' salaries. Second, it improves competitive parity by preventing teams in large markets (e.g., the Dallas Cowboys) from outspending teams in smaller markets (e.g., the Jacksonville Jaguars).

4. Salary Cap Calculation

Per the Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA) between the NFL and the players' union, the salary cap is calculated as a function of NFL revenue. This means that as NFL revenue increases, so does the salary cap. Because the NFL is incredibly good at making money, the salary cap tends to increase year over year. Remember this for later, as it is a large part of how clubs stay under the cap while spending evermore on player salaries.

5. Signing Players

When a player signs with a club, the player signs a contract agreeing to exclusively provide their services to that club. When and how much the player is paid is also determined by the contract. It is worth noting that when a player is paid is often not when that money counts against the team's cap. The amount of a player's compensation that counts against a club's salary cap in a given year is known as a "cap hit."

6. Paragraph 5 Salary

The player's Paragraph 5 salary (named after its position in the standard NFL contract) determines the player's base salary. Add a base salary by dragging the pink " Base Salary" contract element to John Doe's contract and adding his Paragraph 5 salary (in millions) to the input boxes on the first row, with each box representing a different year. The second row shows a player's cap hit per year. Because a player's base salary directly counts against the cap, the two rows are the same. This will not always be the case.

7. Guaranteed Money

Unlike other leagues like the NBA, NFL contracts are not typically fully guaranteed. However, a portion of a player's base salary may be guaranteed. Although there are different types of guarantees, we will skip that nuance. Add a guaranteed salary by dragging the "Guaranteed Salary" contract element to John Doe's contract.

8. Signing Bonuses

Another form of guaranteed money is signing bonuses. As the name suggests, signing bonuses are paid to a player when they sign a contract with a team. Although the signing bonus is paid in full when the contract is signed, its cap hit is prorated, or spread out, over the length of the contract up to 5 years. You can see this in the player's cap hits. Add a signing bonus to the contract and note how the bonus's cap hit is spread out over multiple years even though the player is paid all at once. Keep in mind that you may need to scroll to see all contract elements.

9. Option Bonuses

Option bonuses give the team (or less often, a player) the right to add more years onto a contract in exchange for a bonus. Similarly to Signing Bonuses, Option Bonuses are prorated over the length of the contract up to 5 years.

10. Workout & Roster Bonuses

Workout bonuses are awarded to players who participate in a certain percentage of a team's workouts as stipulated in their contract. Roster bonuses are awarded to players who are on a team's roster at a certain date. Unlike signing and option bonuses, these bonuses are not prorated.

11. Performance Incentives

Performance incentives are awarded to players who meet some statistical measure during a season. They are categorized as either likely or unlikely based on a player's performance last season. If a player achieves a likely incentive, it counts against the cap for that year. If they do not, it still counts against the cap but is refunded next year. If a player achieves an unlikely incentive, it is paid to the player in that year but counts against next year's cap. If a player fails an unlikely incentive, nothing happens. Add an incentive to John Doe's contract and see how changing the type of incentive changes its cap hit. (A "+" indicated money refunded to a club's cap.)

12. Franchise Tags

Franchise tags are one-year contracts that allow a club to retain a certain player. These tend to be very costly for a team to use. They are either exclusive or non-exclusive. Non-exclusive franchise tags are cheaper, but also still allow for the player to leave the club if another club offers a deal bigger than the franchise tag.

13. NFL Rookie Contracts

The NFL draft is held every year in late April or early May. The players taken in the draft also count against a club's salary cap. Rookie contracts are four years and the amount is based on when a player was selected, per the CBA. Players taken in the first round of the draft have a fifth-year team option in their contract.

14. Staying Under the Cap

It is common for NFL clubs to come up against the salary cap. If this happens, they may have to make moves to stay under the cap, including restructuring contracts, trading players, or releasing players. (A player released by a team for cap reasons is known as a "cap casualty.") Because the cap tends to increase, a common tactic is to move money into the future.

15. Restructuring

Teams often need to lower the cap hit of players while wanting to keep them on the team. To do this, clubs change a player's compensation such that it counts against the cap in future years when the cap will be higher. A common way to do this is to convert a portion of a player's base salary into a signing bonus. You can do this by double-clicking on a part of John Doe's base salary and selecting how much you want to convert to a signing bonus. You can also press the "C" key in any part of John Doe's base salary. To see the effect best, pick one of the earlier years. Note how the player's compensation does not change whereas the player's cap hits do.

16. Releasing/Trading a Player

If a team cannot afford to keep a player, they may release or trade them. In cap calculations, releasing and trading a player is the same thing. If this happens, a player does not receive any of their remaining base salary, roster bonuses, or workout bonuses. However, they get to keep their guaranteed money and any signing or option bonuses stay on a club's cap. This money that counts against a club's cap for a player no longer on the team is known as a "dead cap." There are, however, some more considerations that go into calculating this " dead cap."

17. Pre- & Post-June 1st

June 1st is an important date for releasing players. If a player is released on or before June 1st, all their future cap hits accelerate onto the current year. If a player is released after June 1st, their future cap hits accelerate onto the next year. (Teams also can release up to 1 player before June 1st with a "June 1st designation.") Release John Doe by dragging the "Release or Trade" element into their contract.

18. Conclusion

Thank you for taking this guide through the NFL salary cap. Hopefully you now have a much better understanding of the clubs' salary caps and their roster moves to stay in cap compliance.
